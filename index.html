Design rationale & process

The question

The single most important number in climate policy is when — and where — warming crosses a given threshold. We wanted a viewer to feel the geographic and temporal stakes at once: the Arctic is already a different world; the Tropics will be soon; what humans do this decade decides whether anywhere stays below 2°C.

Visual encoding

The hero is a choropleth on an equal-earth projection. Year of first crossing is mapped to a diverging color ramp — early crossings (red/orange) versus late or never (blue/grey). A sequential ramp would imply ordinal severity along one axis; a diverging ramp matches our mental model of "soon vs. eventually vs. avoided".

Position on the map is the natural channel for spatial data, and color ramp for the ordinal year value. We considered animated dots and small multiples (one map per scenario) but chose a single map with toggle controls because the comparison the user actually makes — "what changes when I move from SSP1-2.6 to SSP5-8.5?" — is more visceral when the same view morphs.

Interaction

Three primary controls (scenario, threshold, mode) drive every panel simultaneously. The trajectory line chart shows how the global mean evolves and where the chosen threshold sits relative to all three scenarios — the dot indicating the chosen scenario's crossing year is the bridge between the map and the time-axis.

Click-to-inspect on the map opens a per-grid-cell time series so the user can see the actual warming curve for any spot — Arctic Russia, the Sahel, central Brazil. Hover gives details on demand without committing to a click. A year scrubber animates the anomaly map for users who want a "play" view of warming spreading across the globe.

Data transformations

The CMIP6 CESM2 monthly near-surface air temperature ('tas') field is converted from Kelvin to Celsius, averaged annually, and expressed as anomalies relative to the 2015–2034 mean (we will replace this with a pre-industrial baseline using the historical experiment for the final version). Per grid cell we compute the first year the anomaly equals or exceeds each threshold; cells that never cross within 2015–2100 are rendered as "never". Global and regional means are area-weighted by cos(latitude). The grid is 72×144 (~2.5°), down from CESM2's native resolution to keep the page fast.

Process

Built with D3 v7 — no charting library, all custom shapes. ~30 people-hours so far: data acquisition and cleaning was about a third, the choropleth and projection plumbing another third, and the linked- view choreography and visual polish the last third. Hardest part was making the four panels stay in sync without lag while the user drags the year slider.
