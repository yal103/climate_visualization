Design Rationale & Process

The question

Our visualization focuses on one main question: when do different parts of the world cross major warming thresholds? Instead of only showing a single global average, we wanted viewers to see that climate change is spatially uneven. Some regions pass 2°C much earlier than others, and the difference between scenarios changes the timing of those crossings.

Visual Encoding

We chose a world choropleth map as the central view because our data is geographic. The position on the map represents location, while color represents either the first year a grid cell crosses the selected warming threshold or the temperature anomaly in a selected year. This makes the main pattern easy to read such as the regions that warm earlier or more intensely stand out immediately.

After receiving feedback that our earlier color scale made most of the map look like similar shades of orange/red, we revised the color encoding and switched to a more noticeable color scheme and used binned color categories instead of using the continuous color differences. This makes it easier to distinguish between regions that cross a threshold early, later, or not within the shown time range. The bins also help the legend communicate the data more clearly because viewers can compare categories without needing to interpret very subtle color gradients.

Based on our feedback of focusing on a smaller region or country to reduce visual clutter, we decided to keep the full globe because the central point of the project is global unevenness. A regional view would make local details easier to see, but it would weaken the comparison across the Arctic, tropics, and oceans. To address the concern without losing the global framing, we kept the world map as the main view and added region specific interactions that let users inspect specific places or choose specific regions for closer comparison.

Interaction

The main interactions we have are scenario selection, threshold selection, map mode selection, hover tooltips, click-to-inspect, region chips, and a year scrubber. We chose these interactions because they support the main comparison questions: how does the map change across emissions scenarios, how does the answer change for 1.5°C versus 2°C or higher thresholds, and what does the warming curve look like for a specific place?

More specifically, click-to-inspect on the map opens a per-grid-cell time series so the user can see the actual warming curve for any spot — Arctic Russia, the Sahel, central Brazil. Hover gives details on demand without committing to a click. A year scrubber animates the anomaly map for users who want a "play" view of warming spreading across the globe.

Data Transformations

The CMIP6 CESM2 monthly near-surface air temperature ('tas') field is converted from Kelvin to Celsius, averaged annually, and expressed as anomalies relative to the 2015–2034 baseline. Per grid cell we compute the first year the anomaly equals or exceeds each threshold; cells that never cross within 2015–2100 are rendered as "never". Global and regional means are area-weighted by cos(latitude). The grid is 72×144 (~2.5°), down from CESM2's native resolution to keep the page fast.

Process

Our team split the work by task. YangYang focused on loading the data into a simple html file for all of us to build on it to create our total of 6 visualizations. We each did our own data cleaning and transformation to make our 3-4 visualizations per person and then we chose the top 6 visualizations that helped us develop our dynamic visualization. YangYang and Rocio created seperate demos to test out our d3 visualization and then we merged our ideas to the final website seen today. Hoang implemented the writeup on the final website and then Rocio updated it to represent the team's accomplishments. We spent roughly 15-20 people-hours developing the application. The most time-consuming parts were preparing the climate data into a browser-friendly format, getting the map and charts to update together smoothly, and revising the color scale after feedback. The linked interactions were also challenging because changing one control needed to update multiple views without making the page feel slow or inconsistent.

Limitations

The Mercator Map was used as our primary map visual to represent the changes in temperature on the globe. This map has its limitations where the area ratios of countries are significantly distorted towards the poles. This leads to countries at higher latitudes appearing much larger on the map than they actually are. This matters for climate data because polar regions appear visually larger than they are, which can make high- latitude warming look more dominant on the map. We tried to reduce this issue by using area-weighted global and regional summaries in the charts, so the statistics are not based on the distorted visual area of the map. The use of the Mercator Map was a design choice to provide a familiar and widely recognized representation of the world, but it's important to acknowledge its limitations in accurately representing the world.

Another limitation is that a global map can still feel visually dense, especially when many regions cross thresholds around similar years. Our updated binned color scheme improves readability, but it also simplifies the data by grouping nearby years together. We chose this tradeoff because the purpose of the map is to communicate broad timing patterns clearly, while the tooltip and selected-region time series provide more detailed values when the viewer wants them.
